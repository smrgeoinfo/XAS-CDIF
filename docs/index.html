collimation

https://w3id.org/cdif/xas/collimation
Definition
How beam collimation is provided to limit the incident x-ray beam to the required dimensions and angular spread.
Note
beamline property, describe the arrangement of absorbers used for collimation (https://doi.org/10.1351/goldbook.C01154). From XDI dictionary 'A concise statement of how beam collimation is provided' . A collimated beam of electromagnetic radiation has parallel rays, and therefore will spread minimally as it propagates. (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collimated_beam). Collimators are divided according to shape, energy, spatial resolution and sensitivity of collimator. This is determined by the thickness of collimator, hole density, and thickness of partitions. According to shape collimators can be: --- multi-hole collimator with parallel holes: collimator with thousands of holes, which are in a parallel axis with detector. Detector image has the same size. This is the most often used collimator. --- divergent collimator: collimator with holes diverging towards the radiation source. It allows to gain bigger picture than surface of crystal, but it is smaller sensitivity and spatial resolution-It takes up an area larger than the area of the detector. --- convergent collimator: collimator with holes converging to radiation source. That´s what we get enlarged picture of little organ with an increased sensitivity and a spatilal resolution-It spreads a small area of the object over a larger area of the detector. --- pinhole collimator: funnel-shaped collimator with diametr hole 3-5 mm. It provides enlarged picture wit the highest positional resolution, but there is low sensitivity (extends examination time). It is extended time of examination. It is inteded for observation of small organs (for example, thyroid gland, testicles). --- fan beam collimator: this type has focus in transversal direction while it is parallel in axial direction; has more than 50% higher spatial resolution than parallel type. It is used in cerebral computer tomography. Thickness of the collimator varies in line with the energy of the emmiter. A low energy collimator paired with a high energy emmiter would leak photons an cause blur in the signal (https://www.wikilectures.eu/w/Collimator, https://doi.org/10.1351/goldbook.C01155)

edge analyzed

https://w3id.org/cdif/xas/edgeanalyzed
Definition
Target absorption edge.
Note
The 'edge' is a sudden increase in x-ray absorption occurring when the energy of incident X-rays is just above the binding energy of the targeted electron shell of the atoms interacting with the photons. vocabulary from XDI spec: {K, L, L1, L2, L3, M, M1, M2, M3, M4, M5, N, N1, N2, N3, N4, N5, N6, N7, O, O1, O2, O3, O4, O5, O6, O7}. Used in XDI and NeXus to refer to the shell of the atom where the electron hole is created (K, L, etc). The absorption edge is defined as K, L, L1, etc. That indicates the electronic energy levels involved in the absorption. The edge_energy tells us how much is the value of the energy of the electronic energy levels involved in that particular absorption edge. see https://xrayabsorption.org/xraytable/
References
https://github.com/XraySpectroscopy/XAS-Data-Interchange/blob/master/specification/dictionary.md

edge_energy

https://w3id.org/cdif/xas/edgeenergy
Definition
The absorption edge as used in the data acquisition software.(XDI dictionary)
Note
An absorption edge is the energy at which there is a sharp rise (discontinuity) in the (linear) absorption coefficient of X-rays by an element, which occurs when the energy of the photon corresponds to the energy of a shell of the atom (K, LI, LII, LIII, etc. corresponding to the creation of electron holes in the 1s, 2s, 2p1/2, 2p3/2 etc. atomic subshells). Associated with this transition is the absorption threshold (q.v.) characterized by the corresponding energy E0. (https://doi.org/10.1351/goldbook.08767, https://dictionary.iucr.org/Absorption_edge). Discussed in team meeting 2025-09-08; general sense in group is that this is a known quantity specified for experiment design, not a measured result.

Monochromator Energy energy

https://w3id.org/cdif/xas/monochromatorenergy
Definition
The energy selected by the X-ray monochromator and delivered to the sample during an absorption scan.
Note
This is the independent variable in an XAS scan. NEXUS comment is 'energy selected'. An energy monochromator is a critical component that selects a specific, monochromatic X-ray energy from an X-ray beam. Notes: Typically a Bragg crystal monochromator is used. This energy is continuously tunable and determines the energy at which absorption measurements are performed, enabling precise probing of core-level electronic transitions near an element’s absorption edge. In XAS, monochromator energy is critical for scanning across absorption edges to extract fine structural information such as XANES and EXAFS. The energy is selected using Bragg’s Law: lambda = 2d*sin(theta) $$ where lambda is the X-ray wavelength, dd is the crystal lattice spacing, and theta is the angle of incidence. The Incident Energy variable reports the measured energy delivered to the sample. (International Tables for Crystallography, Vol. I, Chapter 3.4 )
foaf:focus

Note
Monochromator crystal property. It’s not a physical surface, but a mathematical construct based on the periodic arrangement of atoms. A classic example of a reflection plane in crystallography is the (100) plane in a cubic crystal system. Miller index: (100), Interpretation: This plane intersects the x-axis at 1 unit, and is parallel to both the y- and z-axes (i.e., it never intersects them).
